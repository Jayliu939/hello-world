Experiences

NUS research attachment

What it was about

This year-long research attachment involved me and two other classmates, where we were tasked with heating fish scales
and recording the effects of the heat, in order to make them adsorb oil so that they can be used in kitchen sinks
to collect oil and turn them into biofuel. The principle behind this was that the collagen in fish scales would expand during heating,
allowing oil to be adsorbed. In order to carry out this goal, we first characterised the fishscales by
exposing them to various types of light then noting the fluorescence, as well as analysing them using FTIR. I personally was also
responsible for the design of a prototype water filter to put in our treated fish scales to prove our hypothesis. Before then,
I also had to simulate water flow through the filter to ensure maximum adsorption took place.

My takeaways from this experience

Frankly, the result was quite disappointing - we did not manage to get into the SSEF finals. However, I still felt that
the experience was undoubtedly one of the best I ever had; Few ever got to be in a real university lab in secondary school,
much less get to conduct their own scientific investigation and write a paper. This experience definitely reinforced my
mental fortitude as it was very draining to get through all the failures and the challenge of balancing school work with
the mountains of reference papers we had to read through.

Science Buskers

Science Buskers is a competition organised by Science Center in which competitors try to explain scientific concepts
in a way that people of all backgrounds could understand. My team, composed of one classmate in our first attempt and
with two in our second attempt. In our first attempt, our demonstration was showing how purple cabbage juice acted like a indicator.
However, the project was quite rushed as many of our previous attempts involving different experiments did not succeed, meaning that
we did not have enough time to practice our script.

For our next year, the demonstration we choose was the iodine clock experiment. Although I felt that our preperation was sufficient this
time around, I thought our presentation skills could still be improved as we occasionally stuttered and forgot a few lines.
Despite this, I felt that I had learnt alot from these two events as I learnt more about the importance of being well-prepared
especially when it came to presentation. It also taught me to perservere in the face of adversity as we faced many challenges during both attempts.

My role in NCC

Aeromodelling is a division of NCC which is unique to air branches. In aeromodelling, cadets get to build wooden gliders
and fly drones. As the head of Aeromodelling, I was responsible for the planning and execution of aeromod sessions.
This includes writing the timeplan (which is a timeline of events that will happen in the session, as well as the
responsiblities/roles of supporting personnel and the logistics required. Not only that, whenever the Excos were unable
to turn up for CCA, I would take over their responsiblities of ensuring the training went smoothly.

Qualities and experiences I have gained from NCC

As an aeromodelling head, I have gained a wealth of knowledge from NCC. Most prominent of which was learning to be more adaptible.
One of the more memoriable incidences that took place was when our original training location was notified to us to be off-limits

reorganise the entire training plan on the spot. After 20 minutes running from venue to venue, breifing the
other cadet leaders on the new arrangements, I finally managed to get the session on track. In the end, everything went
smoothly afterwards and we even finished ahead of schedule.
I graduated from that year's NCC batch as the only Staff-Sergeant, just one rank below exco.
